# Communication Value Delivery

## Purpose

Help an agent turn a capability, feature, automation, product, service, or internal initiative into clear value communication that stakeholders can understand, test, trust, and adopt.

This skill focuses on four connected outcomes:

- Communicate benefits in audience-specific language.
- Create demos and use cases that make value concrete.
- Explain ROI, assumptions, risks, and confidence clearly.
- Gather feedback, iterate, and drive adoption through trust-building actions.

## When to use

Use this skill when the user asks to:

- Explain the value of a product, automation, feature, process change, service, or internal initiative.
- Create demo scripts, demo agendas, proof-of-value plans, use cases, pilot plans, stakeholder walkthroughs, or launch narratives.
- Translate technical capabilities into business benefits, user outcomes, executive summaries, enablement content, or adoption messaging.
- Build an ROI story, value model, assumption register, benefits tracker, or impact summary.
- Gather stakeholder feedback, synthesize feedback themes, prioritize improvements, and plan iterations.
- Improve adoption, trust, credibility, buy-in, change readiness, or user engagement.

## Do not use

Do not use this skill when:

- The user asks for deceptive, manipulative, or pressure-based persuasion.
- The output would require fabricated metrics, false customer claims, fake testimonials, or unsupported ROI claims.
- The user needs final financial, legal, compliance, procurement, or regulatory approval instead of a communication or planning aid.
- The task is unrelated to value communication, demos, feedback, adoption, or stakeholder trust.
- The task requires exposing private customer data, confidential metrics, or personal data without authorization.

## Required inputs

Use the information already available. Ask for missing details only when essential; otherwise make reasonable assumptions and label them.

Recommended inputs:

- Initiative, capability, product, feature, process, or automation being communicated.
- Target audience, stakeholder group, or buyer/user persona.
- Desired behavior change or adoption goal.
- Current pain point, baseline workflow, or status quo.
- Benefits, evidence, metrics, user quotes, pilot findings, or expected outcomes.
- Costs, effort, constraints, risks, dependencies, or change impacts.
- Demo environment, available data, and any limitations that must be disclosed.
- Feedback channels, review cadence, launch timeline, and success measures.
- Required output format, such as demo script, use-case library, ROI narrative, launch plan, FAQ, or feedback synthesis.

## Workflow

1. Confirm the communication goal and adoption objective.
   - Identify what the audience should understand, believe, decide, try, approve, or adopt.
   - Name the decision stage: awareness, evaluation, pilot, approval, rollout, renewal, or expansion.

2. Segment the audience.
   - List stakeholder groups, their priorities, likely objections, trust concerns, and preferred proof.
   - Translate one generic benefit into audience-specific outcomes.

3. Build the value message.
   - Use this structure: for the audience, who has the pain, the initiative helps achieve the outcome by the mechanism, supported by the proof, with these guardrails.
   - Separate benefits into user value, business value, operational value, risk reduction, and strategic value.
   - Avoid claims that are not supported by evidence or clearly labeled assumptions.

4. Create use cases.
   - For each use case, capture user, trigger, current pain, new workflow, before/after contrast, value, proof, limitations, and next step.
   - Prioritize use cases by impact, feasibility, urgency, visibility, and fit with adoption goals.

5. Design a clear demo.
   - Start with the audience's problem, not the feature list.
   - Show a realistic before state, the changed workflow, and the aha moment.
   - Include one trust-building moment that explains what the demo does not prove, what data was used, and what must be validated next.
   - End with a specific call to action: approve a pilot, test a workflow, review assumptions, nominate users, or provide feedback.

6. Explain ROI and value.
   - Define baseline, expected change, adoption volume, time saved, cost avoided, revenue enabled, risk reduced, quality improved, and confidence level.
   - Show formulas and assumptions before conclusions.
   - Use ranges when inputs are uncertain.
   - Include qualitative value when direct financial conversion would be misleading.

7. Plan adoption and trust-building.
   - Identify champions, skeptics, enablement needs, change impacts, training moments, governance, and support channels.
   - Create transparent messaging for risks, limitations, data privacy, model or process reliability, and human oversight.
   - Provide a rollout sequence: pilot, learn, refine, enable, scale, measure.

8. Gather feedback.
   - Choose feedback methods: interviews, survey, office hours, in-product prompts, usage data, support tickets, stakeholder reviews, or retrospective workshops.
   - Ask about clarity, credibility, usefulness, usability, objections, missing use cases, blockers, and desired proof.
   - Separate signal from noise by grouping feedback into themes and ranking by adoption impact and effort.

9. Iterate.
   - Convert feedback into changes to the demo, value story, use cases, ROI assumptions, enablement materials, or product/process design.
   - Track decisions, open questions, and next validation steps.

10. Package the output.
    - Provide the requested deliverable with assumptions, evidence gaps, recommended next step, and feedback loop.
    - Make the output usable by the stated audience without extra explanation.

## Output format

Return the requested artifact. Common formats include:

- Value proposition and benefit map.
- Use-case library or prioritized use-case table.
- Demo agenda, script, talk track, or storyboard.
- ROI narrative, assumptions table, formula, and sensitivity notes.
- Adoption plan, champion plan, stakeholder FAQ, objection handling, or enablement checklist.
- Feedback survey, interview guide, synthesis, prioritization, and iteration plan.

When presenting ROI, always include:

- Inputs and assumptions.
- Calculation method or formula.
- Confidence level.
- Non-financial benefits and risks.
- What must be validated next.

## Quality checklist

Before finalizing:

- The output starts from audience pain and desired outcome, not only features.
- Benefits are concrete, specific, and relevant to the target audience.
- Demos show a realistic scenario, clear aha moment, and next step.
- Use cases include before/after contrast and proof or validation needs.
- ROI claims are traceable to assumptions, formulas, or evidence.
- Unsupported claims are labeled as assumptions or removed.
- Feedback questions can produce actionable iteration decisions.
- Adoption plan addresses trust, objections, enablement, and measurement.
- Sensitive data, confidential metrics, and private user information are not exposed.

## Safety and privacy

- Do not fabricate results, customer references, usage data, savings, revenue, or endorsements.
- Do not pressure, shame, or manipulate stakeholders into adoption.
- Do not hide limitations, material risks, conflicts of interest, or unresolved validation needs.
- Do not expose confidential data, private customer information, personal data, or proprietary metrics unless explicitly authorized.
- Do not present estimates as audited financial results.
- Do not replace expert review for financial, legal, compliance, medical, safety, security, or regulatory decisions.
